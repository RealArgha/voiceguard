--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 1: WHY CNN AND NOT LSTM OR TRANSFORMER
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WHY NOT LSTM
------------
LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) is a recurrent network. It processes the
spectrogram one time frame at a time, left to right, carrying a hidden state
forward. This is the right design for tasks where the order and evolution of a
sequence matters — speech recognition, language modelling, music generation.
The meaning of what comes next depends on what came before.

Spoofing detection does not work that way. You do not need to remember what
frame 1 sounded like when processing frame 200. The artifact is LOCAL and
REPETITIVE — an unnatural harmonic pattern in one 30ms window looks the same
as the one 2 seconds later. LSTM processes frame by frame and would need to
learn to ignore the sequential structure it was designed to exploit, which is
wasted capacity.

Practical problems with LSTM for this task:

1. Sequential by design — cannot be parallelised across the time axis. On a
   GPU you lose nearly all parallel compute advantage. A CNN processes all 297
   time frames simultaneously in a single matrix multiply.

2. Vanishing gradient across long sequences — even with LSTM gating, 297
   frames is a long sequence. The gradient signal from the last frame barely
   reaches the first frame during backpropagation.

3. Much larger parameter count for equivalent performance — a single LSTM
   layer with hidden size 256 on a 128-dim input has
   4 × (128 + 256) × 256 = ~393,000 parameters just for one layer.
   The entire VoiceGuardCNN has only ~180,000 parameters total.

4. Variable-length handling is awkward — LSTMs need padding and masking for
   variable-length inputs. The CNN uses Global Average Pool which handles any
   length natively with zero extra code.

One case where LSTM beats CNN for audio: speaker verification, where the model
needs to aggregate identity information across a full utterance and compare it
to a reference. That is genuinely sequential — who someone is depends on how
their voice evolves. Whether a voice is synthetic does not.


WHY NOT TRANSFORMER
-------------------
Transformers address LSTM's sequential bottleneck by replacing recurrence with
self-attention: every frame attends to every other frame in parallel. This is
why transformers beat LSTMs on almost every sequence task since 2018.

But for this task specifically, self-attention creates a new problem: it learns
which frames to pay attention to, which implies the task requires relating
distant frames. TTS artifacts do not require cross-frame reasoning. You do not
need to know what happened at frame 50 to classify frame 200. The artifact is
in the local texture. Self-attention on 297 frames computes
297 × 297 = 88,209 attention weights per head per layer — a lot of computation
to learn "every frame is equally relevant."

The CNN's inductive bias — local connectivity via 3×3 kernels, translation
invariance via MaxPool — is perfectly matched to local texture detection. This
inductive bias means the CNN learns the right thing with far less data and far
fewer parameters. Transformers have almost no inductive bias, which is why they
need millions of examples to reach their potential. The training set here is
~640,000 clips — enough for CNN, borderline for a transformer.

SECTION 2: WHAT THE MODEL IS LEARNING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The CNN is learning to distinguish two kinds of log-mel spectrograms:

BONAFIDE (real human voice):
- Natural pitch jitter and micro-tremor in vocal folds
- Irregular harmonic structure — harmonics are never perfectly spaced
- Breath noise between words
- Slightly uneven formant transitions
- Spectral texture is rough and organic

SPOOF (synthetic / TTS voice):
- Produced by neural vocoders: WaveNet, HiFiGAN, VITS
- Unnaturally smooth formant trajectories
- Overly clean, perfectly spaced harmonics
- Phase patterns that no human larynx produces
- Subtle periodic artifacts from the vocoder's upsampling architecture
- Spectral texture is smooth and mechanical

The CNN never "knows" any of this explicitly. It sees thousands of labelled
spectrograms and gradient descent finds the 3×3 filters that best separate
the two classes. What emerges in the filters is the network's own
representation of "real texture" vs "synthetic texture."


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 3: INPUT REPRESENTATION — LOG-MEL SPECTROGRAM
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Raw audio cannot be fed directly to a CNN. 48,000 numbers per second of PCM
is just a pressure waveform — there is no 2D spatial structure for conv layers
to learn from. The spectrogram pipeline converts the 1D waveform into a 2D
image.

STEP A: Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT)
A 512-sample (32ms) window slides across the audio in 160-sample (10ms) steps.
At each position, FFT computes the frequency content of that 32ms slice.
For 3 seconds of audio: (48000 - 512) / 160 + 1 ≈ 297 windows.
Each window produces 257 frequency bins.
Raw spectrogram shape: (257, 297) — frequencies × time frames.

STEP B: Mel Filterbank
The 257 linear FFT bins are compressed into 128 mel bins using triangular
filters spaced on the mel scale. Human hearing is logarithmic — we distinguish
pitches much better at low frequencies than high. The mel scale matches this.

More importantly, TTS artifacts appear as unnatural smoothness in mel space
because neural vocoders (HiFiGAN, WaveNet) are explicitly designed around mel
spectrograms. The artifacts are most visible in the representation the vocoders
themselves use.

Output shape: (128, 297)

STEP C: Power to dB
librosa.power_to_db(mel, ref=np.max)
Converts energy to decibels: 10 × log10(mel / max_value).
Compresses dynamic range so quiet and loud audio produce comparable patterns.
Without this, quiet audio has near-zero values and the CNN ignores quiet frames.

STEP D: Normalize
log_mel = (log_mel - log_mel.mean()) / (log_mel.std() + 1e-6)
Zero-mean, unit-variance normalization. Ensures the CNN sees the same input
range regardless of mic volume, background noise level, or recording equipment.

FINAL SHAPE: (128, 297) float32 numpy array.
This is a grayscale image: 128 pixels tall (frequency), 297 pixels wide (time).
Dark pixels = high energy. Light pixels = low energy.

CRITICAL RULE: These exact parameters — n_fft=512, hop_length=160, n_mels=128,
sr=16000 — must be IDENTICAL during training and inference. If they differ
by even one parameter, the CNN sees completely different patterns and breaks.

SECTION 4: MODEL ARCHITECTURE — VoiceGuardCNN
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Input: (B, 1, 128, 297)   — batch × 1 channel × 128 mel bins × 297 time frames

BLOCK 1:
  Conv2d(in=1, out=32, kernel=3×3, padding=1)  →  (B, 32, 128, 297)
  BatchNorm2d(32)                               →  normalise per channel
  ReLU(inplace=True)                            →  zero out negatives
  MaxPool2d(kernel=2)                           →  (B, 32, 64, 148)

BLOCK 2:
  Conv2d(in=32, out=64, kernel=3×3, padding=1)  →  (B, 64, 64, 148)
  BatchNorm2d(64)
  ReLU
  MaxPool2d(2)                                   →  (B, 64, 32, 74)

BLOCK 3:
  Conv2d(in=64, out=128, kernel=3×3, padding=1)  →  (B, 128, 32, 74)
  BatchNorm2d(128)
  ReLU
  MaxPool2d(2)                                    →  (B, 128, 16, 37)

Global Average Pool: AdaptiveAvgPool2d(1,1)       →  (B, 128, 1, 1)
Flatten                                            →  (B, 128)
FC: Linear(128, 1)                                 →  (B, 1)
Squeeze                                            →  (B,)
Sigmoid                                            →  (B,)  — values in [0, 1]

Output: P(synthetic) — probability this clip is AI-generated.
Threshold at 0.56: above = synthetic, below = real.

WHY EACH PIECE EXISTS:

3×3 Conv: Smallest kernel that captures 2D local patterns. Padding=1 keeps
spatial dimensions unchanged through the conv (only MaxPool halves them).
Standard choice since VGG (2014).

BatchNorm: Normalises activations within each batch — massively stabilises
training and acts as regularisation. For each of the 32 channels in block1,
computes mean and variance across the 256-sample batch and normalises.
Learnable γ (scale) and β (shift) let the network undo normalisation if needed.
Without BatchNorm, training on 640k clips would have severe gradient instability.

ReLU(inplace=True): Non-linearity. f(x) = max(0, x). Simple, no vanishing
gradient problem. inplace=True saves memory by modifying the tensor in place
rather than allocating a new one.

MaxPool(2×2): Takes the maximum value in each 2×2 window. Halves spatial
dimensions (manages computation) and makes features translation-invariant.
If a harmonic artifact is at position (40, 100) or (41, 101), MaxPool returns
the same output. After 3 blocks: 128 mel bins → 16, 297 frames → 37.

Global Average Pool (GAP): Takes the mean of each feature map across all
spatial positions. This is the key design decision. A fixed-size FC after
flattening would require every clip to be exactly the same length. GAP produces
a 128-dim vector from any spatial size — the model handles variable-length
audio natively. A 2-second clip and a 5-second clip both produce a 128-vector.

FC(128→1) + Sigmoid: Single output neuron. Sigmoid squashes to [0,1],
interpreted as probability of being synthetic.

Total parameters: ~180,000
Model file size: 0.4 MB

SECTION 5: PHASE 1 TRAINING — ASVspoof 2019 LA
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

DATASET:
  25,380 audio clips total
  - 2,580 bonafide (real human speakers, studio recorded)
  - 22,800 spoof (13 different attack systems: TTS + voice conversion)

The 13 attack systems in 2019 LA include:
  - Waveform concatenation (old-school TTS)
  - Statistical parametric synthesis (HMM-based)
  - Neural TTS (early WaveNet-style systems)
  - Voice conversion systems

This gives the model a broad base of synthetic patterns from 2019-era TTS.

TRAINING CONFIGURATION:
  Loss:       BCELoss (Binary Cross Entropy)
  Optimiser:  AdamW, lr=1e-3, weight_decay=1e-4
  Epochs:     8
  Batch size: 128
  Augment:    optional (--augment flag)

PURPOSE: Teaches the model what TTS artifacts look like in clean studio
recordings. This is the base model that subsequent training builds on.

SECTION 6: PHASE 2 TRAINING — COMBINED 2019 + 2021
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WHY A SECOND TRAINING PHASE:
The 2019 dataset uses studio recordings. By 2021, TTS systems had advanced
significantly — WaveGlow, HiFiGAN, VITS produce voices nearly indistinguishable
to human ears. Also, the 2021 eval set includes real telephone channel
conditions: codec compression, channel noise, bandwidth limitation. The 2019
model fails on these because it learned features specific to clean studio audio.

COMBINED DATASET:
  2019 LA train:   ~25,000 clips (full, from ASVSPOOF_ROOT)
  2021 LA eval:    181,566 clips available, capped at 60,000 for training time
  2021 DF eval:    458,874 clips across 3 parts, capped at 60,000
  2021 PA eval:    600,000+ clips, capped at 60,000
  Total training:  ~205,000 clips per run (stratified caps applied)
  Validation:      2019 LA dev, ~24,844 clips (never seen during training)

DATASET TYPES:
  LA (Logical Access): Digital TTS and voice conversion attacks
  DF (DeepFake):       More recent neural deepfake systems
  PA (Physical Access): Replay attacks — recording a real voice and playing it
                        back through a speaker. Different artifact profile.

PROTOCOL PARSING:
  2019 format: col[1]=file_id  col[4]=label (bonafide|spoof)
  2021 LA/DF:  col[1]=file_id  col[5]=label
  2021 PA:     col[1]=file_id  col[9]=label (different column — PA has more fields)

TRAINING CONFIGURATION:
  Loss:       BCELoss
  Optimiser:  AdamW, lr=5e-4, weight_decay=1e-4
  Epochs:     10
  Batch size: 256 (fits on RTX 4070 with AMP)
  Augment:    strongly recommended (--augment flag)
  Schedule:   warmup (1 epoch linear ramp) + cosine annealing

TRANSFER LEARNING:
  Starts from voiceguard_cnn_aug.pt (best 2019 checkpoint).
  Conv blocks already know audio representations — they have learned edges,
  harmonic structures, noise textures from 25k 2019 clips. The 2021 training
  fine-tunes these representations to cover newer TTS systems and telephone
  channel conditions without starting from scratch.

RESULT: voiceguard_cnn_2021.pt — 1.41% EER on 2021 eval set.

SECTION 7: STEP-BY-STEP TRAINING LOOP
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

STEP 1 — AUDIO LOADING
  audio, _ = librosa.load(path, sr=16000, mono=True)
  Decodes FLAC, resamples to 16000 Hz, converts to mono float32 in [-1, 1].
  8kHz telephone recordings get upsampled to 16kHz.

STEP 2 — PAD OR CROP TO EXACTLY 3 SECONDS
  if len(audio) < 48000: pad with zeros at the end
  else: crop to first 48000 samples
  Every sample must produce identical spectrogram shape for fixed batch dims.

STEP 3 — CACHING
  First seen: compute mel spectrogram → save as .npy file on disk
  Subsequent epochs: load .npy directly (100× faster than running librosa again)
  With --augment: cache RAW AUDIO instead (augmented audio is different every
  epoch so mel cannot be pre-cached — audio is cached, mel recomputed each time)

STEP 4 — AUGMENTATION (applied per-sample at runtime if --augment)

  Audio-level augmentation:

  a) Random gain (60% probability):
     audio = audio × uniform(0.4, 1.4)
     Teaches the model that volume is irrelevant — only spectral shape matters.

  b) Gaussian noise (50% probability, SNR 10-30 dB):
     sig_power = mean(audio²)
     noise_power = sig_power / (10 ^ (snr_db / 10))
     noise = randn(len(audio)) × sqrt(noise_power)
     audio = audio + noise
     SNR 10 dB = quite noisy (loud cafe). SNR 30 dB = clean room.
     Forces the model to find artifacts through background noise.

  c) Low-pass filter (35% probability, cutoff 3.5–7.5 kHz):
     4th-order Butterworth filter applied via scipy.
     Simulates laptop/earbud mic frequency response rolloff.
     Without this, the model learns high-frequency features that do not
     survive real microphone capture.

  d) Phone codec simulation (40% probability):
     Downsample to 8000 Hz or 11025 Hz → upsample back to 16000 Hz.
     Simulates G.711 and narrow-band phone codecs.
     All content above 4 kHz is destroyed. Aliasing artifacts appear.
     The model learns to detect synthetic speech through this degradation.
     MOST IMPORTANT augmentation for phone call use cases.

  Spectrogram-level augmentation (SpecAugment, applied after mel):

  e) Frequency masking (per each of 2 masks):
     Zero out a random band of up to 20 consecutive mel bins.
     Filled with the mean value of the entire spectrogram.

  f) Time masking (per each of 2 masks):
     Zero out a random window of up to 30 consecutive time frames.
     Filled with the mean value.

  Combined, SpecAugment forces the CNN to learn distributed features.
  If it relies on one frequency band and that band is masked, it must find
  the artifact elsewhere. Results in much more robust feature detectors.

STEP 5 — CLASS BALANCING (WeightedRandomSampler)
  The dataset is ~1:8 bonafide:spoof ratio.
  Without correction, the model learns to always predict "spoof" and achieves
  ~89% accuracy while having 50% EER — completely useless.

  sample_weight[bonafide] = 1 / count_bonafide    (large weight, rare class)
  sample_weight[spoof]    = 1 / count_spoof       (small weight, common class)

  WeightedRandomSampler draws each batch using these weights so every mini-batch
  is approximately 50% bonafide + 50% spoof, regardless of the true ratio.
  Equivalent to oversampling the minority class, implemented at batch level.

STEP 6 — DATALOADER CONFIGURATION
  batch_size=256:           256 samples per gradient update
  num_workers=4:            4 CPU threads prefetch data while GPU trains
  pin_memory=True:          loads into non-pageable RAM for fast GPU DMA transfer
  persistent_workers=True:  workers stay alive between epochs (faster restarts)

  Without num_workers, the GPU would idle every batch waiting for data.
  pin_memory gives ~20% speedup on data transfer to GPU.

STEP 7 — FORWARD PASS
  x: (256, 1, 128, 297) on GPU (float16 inside autocast)

  Through block1: (256, 32, 64, 148)   — 32 filters detect low-level patterns
  Through block2: (256, 64, 32, 74)    — 64 filters detect mid-level patterns
  Through block3: (256, 128, 16, 37)   — 128 filters detect high-level patterns
  GAP:            (256, 128)           — aggregate spatial info
  FC + Sigmoid:   (256,)               — 256 independent probability predictions

  Each 3×3 conv filter learns one specific spectral-texture pattern.
  The 128 filters in block3 each detect one type of higher-level artifact.

STEP 8 — LOSS COMPUTATION
  loss = BCELoss(predictions, true_labels)

  For a real voice clip (y=0):
    loss = -log(1 - p)
    If p=0.1 (correctly predicts real): loss = 0.105
    If p=0.9 (wrongly predicts fake):   loss = 2.303  — heavy penalty

  For a synthetic clip (y=1):
    loss = -log(p)
    If p=0.9 (correctly predicts fake): loss = 0.105
    If p=0.1 (misses the fake):         loss = 2.303  — heavy penalty

  The gradient of this loss pushes the model to output values close to 1.0
  for synthetic clips and close to 0.0 for real clips.

STEP 9 — AUTOMATIC MIXED PRECISION (AMP)
  with autocast(device_type="cuda"):
      pred = model(x)                    # runs in float16 on Tensor Cores
  loss = loss_fn(pred.float(), y)        # cast to float32 for numerics

  scaler.scale(loss).backward()          # multiply loss by scale factor before backprop
  scaler.step(opt)                       # unscale gradients, then update weights
  scaler.update()                        # adjust scale factor for next iteration

  float16 runs ~2× faster on RTX 4070 Tensor Cores and uses half the VRAM.
  But float16 has limited precision — very small gradients underflow to zero.
  GradScaler multiplies the loss by ~65536 before backprop so small gradients
  stay representable in float16, then divides back before the optimiser step.

STEP 10 — BACKWARD PASS
  loss.backward()
  PyTorch traces the forward pass in a computation graph. Backward walk applies
  the chain rule at every operation: ∂loss/∂w = ∂loss/∂output × ∂output/∂w
  Every weight in every layer gets its gradient computed simultaneously.
  Gradient = average over all 256 samples in the batch.

STEP 11 — WEIGHT UPDATE (AdamW)
  AdamW maintains per-parameter running statistics:
    m = 0.9 × m + 0.1 × gradient               (exponential moving average of gradient)
    v = 0.999 × v + 0.001 × gradient²           (exponential moving average of gradient²)
    param = param - lr × m / (sqrt(v) + 1e-8)   (adaptive learning rate per parameter)
    param = param × (1 - lr × weight_decay)      (weight decay — L2 regularisation)

  Parameters with consistently large gradients (actively trained) get smaller
  effective LR. Parameters with small gradients get larger effective LR.
  This means all layers train simultaneously even if their contributions differ.

  weight_decay=1e-4 slowly pushes weights toward zero, preventing any single
  weight from growing very large and overfitting to training-set artifacts.

STEP 12 — LEARNING RATE SCHEDULE

  Warmup phase (epoch 0, linear ramp):
    LR starts at lr/10. Ramps linearly to full lr by end of epoch 0.
    Critical for transfer learning — immediately applying full LR to pretrained
    weights produces large gradients that destroy the pretrained representations.
    Warmup lets the optimiser's momentum terms initialise before large updates.

  Cosine annealing (epochs 1–9):
    LR follows 0.5 × (1 + cos(π × t)) from 1.0 to 0.0 (scaled by base lr).
    The cosine shape means LR stays high for most of training (allowing large
    updates) and only drops sharply near the end (gently settling into a
    sharp minimum). Consistently better than linear decay or step schedules.

STEP 13 — VALIDATION AND CHECKPOINT SAVING
  After each epoch, run on 2019 dev set (never seen during training):

  Sweep threshold from 0 to 1:
    At each threshold compute:
      FAR (False Acceptance Rate) = real voices classified as synthetic
      FRR (False Rejection Rate)  = synthetic voices classified as real
    EER = the threshold where FAR equals FRR. Lower is better.

  if val_EER < best_EER:
      save model state dict to weights/voiceguard_cnn_2021.pt

  Only the BEST EER checkpoint is saved, not the last epoch. Training loss
  keeps decreasing (memorisation of training data) but val EER can plateau or
  slightly worsen. Saving the best is implicit early stopping.

  RESULT: 1.41% EER — at the optimal threshold, 1.41% of real voices are
  wrongly flagged AND 1.41% of synthetic voices are missed.

SECTION 8: FINE-TUNING — PERSONAL MIC ADAPTATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

THE DOMAIN GAP PROBLEM:
The 2021 model achieves 1.41% EER on benchmark data but miscalibrates on live
microphone input. Real voice was scoring CNN mean=0.470 (fine) but max=0.565 —
dangerously close to the 0.56 threshold and causing false positives.

The reason: ASVspoof, even with augmentation, does not capture the exact
acoustic fingerprint of your specific EarPods + room + voice. The spectrogram
statistics are slightly different from anything in the training set.

THE DATASET — 143 clips total:
  data/mini/real/     — 105 clips: your own voice recorded via EarPods mic
  data/mini/fake/     — 38 clips: strong TTS clips scoring ≥ 0.60 on 2021 model
  data/mini/fake_weak/ — 26 quarantined clips: TTS scoring < 0.60

WHY WEAK FAKES ARE QUARANTINED:
If you train on fakes the model already finds ambiguous (scores below 0.60),
you teach the model nothing — the model's boundary does not move. Worse, these
borderline fakes pull the decision boundary toward real voice territory. Only
confident fakes (score ≥ 0.60) are included in training because they represent
examples the model can clearly learn to separate.

FINE-TUNING CONFIGURATION:
  Base weights: voiceguard_cnn_2021.pt
  Loss:         BCELoss
  LR phase 1:   1e-4
  LR phase 2:   1e-5 (10× lower)
  Epochs:       15 total (0 warmup, all full-model)
  Augmentation: disabled (--no-augment) — with only 143 clips, augmentation
                can distort the specific mic characteristics you are trying
                to teach the model. The clips ARE the distribution.
  Batch size:   dynamic (min 8, max 32, ~quarter of training set size)
  Split:        80% train / 20% validation (fixed seed=42)

PROGRESSIVE UNFREEZING:

PHASE 1 — FC LAYER ONLY (warmup epochs, 0 in best run):
  for name, param in model.named_parameters():
      param.requires_grad = ("fc" in name)

  Only 129 parameters (128 weights + 1 bias) are updated. The conv blocks are
  completely frozen — their pretrained filters are preserved exactly. This finds
  the best decision boundary for your mic's spectrogram statistics without
  touching the feature extractors.

  When warmup=0 (best settings), this phase is skipped entirely.

PHASE 2 — ALL LAYERS:
  for param in model.parameters():
      param.requires_grad = True
  lr2 = lr × 0.1    # 10× lower than phase 1 lr

  All conv blocks fine-tune gently to match your mic's acoustic fingerprint.
  The 10× lower LR is critical — it prevents catastrophic forgetting.
  At full LR, 143 clips would completely overwrite what 640k clips taught
  the conv blocks. At 0.1× LR, the conv filters shift slightly without losing
  their ability to detect synthetic audio in general.

GRADIENT CLIPPING:
  torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), max_norm=1.0)

  With only 143 clips, gradients can spike wildly on any single bad batch.
  Clipping to norm 1.0 caps the magnitude of the gradient vector before the
  optimiser step, preventing one bad batch from destroying the model.
  Applied every step in both phases.

RESULT: voiceguard_cnn_finetuned.pt — 0% EER on personal validation set.
  Real voice mean CNN:  0.470 → fused score ~28   → LOW  ✓
  Real voice max CNN:   0.565 → fused score ~33.9  → LOW  ✓ (at the edge)
  Fake TTS min CNN:     0.561 → fused score ~33.7  → LOW  (borderline)
  Fake TTS mean CNN:    0.640 → fused score ~38.4  → MEDIUM ✓
  Strong TTS CNN:       0.880 → fused score ~52.8  → HIGH ✓

SECTION 9: SCORE FUSION — FROM CNN TO FINAL RISK SCORE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The CNN outputs a single number: cnn_prob in [0, 1].
This is combined with keyword detection and metadata flags:

  cnn_component     = cnn_prob × 100
  keyword_component = min(keyword_hits / 3, 1.0) × 100
  md_component      = flag-based (unknown_number +50, voip_origin +30,
                      foreign_country +20), capped at 100

  score = 0.60 × cnn_component
        + 0.30 × keyword_component
        + 0.10 × md_component

BAND THRESHOLDS (calibrated to finetuned model + W_CNN=0.60):
  score ≤ 34:   LOW    → PASS           (real voice max is ~33.9)
  34 < score ≤ 45:  MEDIUM → OTP_CHALLENGE
  score > 45:   HIGH   → BLOCK          (VB-Cable TTS at CNN=0.88 → score 52.8)

The thresholds are calibrated so that the maximum real voice score (33.9) sits
just under the LOW boundary, and strong TTS scores land in HIGH. The gap
between real max (33.9) and fake mean (38.4) is the separation margin.
